The curious case of the shared sperm donor

By: Jaclyn Jeffrey-Wilensky

Parents-to-be might choose a sperm donor with attributes, such as eye and hair color, like their own. (Source)

Karen and Julie have a lot in common. They live in the same sleepy East Coast suburb. They have the same coloring—fair skin, blond hair, and light eyes. They even have the same taste in food. (Both favor burgers without buns, so they can eat all their fries without guilt, they say.)

Long before they met, they made the same big leap: they decided to become Single Mothers by Choice, having babies without another parent in the picture. And unbeknownst to them, they chose the same sperm donor to do so.

“Same donor, same county,” Julie said. “It’s just insane.”

The similarities won’t end there. Thanks to the shared donor, their children—Karen’s 9-month-old daughter Nellie and Julie’s future children—will be half-siblings.

By a twist of fate, Julie, Karen, Nellie, and any future children have become a new kind of family, made possible by sperm donation and other related technology.

“The world is changing, and families are not the same as when we grew up,” Julie said. “There is really no norm anymore.”

The phenomenon of donor siblings is nothing new, according to Wendy Kramer, founder of the Donor Sibling Registry, a website that keeps track of children born from the same sperm donor. The Donor Sibling Registry lists thousands of children with donor siblings, and its roster keeps growing.

But to those less familiar with the process, it seems like an insane coincidence. What are the odds that, out of all the sperm donor choices in the world, two women will choose the exact same donor?

If the women go to the same sperm bank, like Karen and Julie did, then you could calculate the probability based on the number of available donors. The sperm bank they used had about 400 donors, giving the women a 1/160000 chance of choosing the same sperm donor.

But that’s assuming that the women chose donors randomly. Karen and Julie both say they didn’t.

“I wanted someone who look like someone that I would have date dated naturally,” Karen said. “Or someone that would belong in my family or look like they would fit in with my family.”

Julie expressed similar sentiments. Both women picked donors who looked like them: white, with light hair and eyes.

Based just on these criteria, the odds grow from 1/160000 to 1/1225—still steep, but more plausible. And after they’d narrowed down those donors by other factors, like height, education, health history, and openness to meeting donor children down the road, there were so few donors who met those criteria that they were actually pretty likely to pick the same donor.

This narrowing effect would be even more dramatic for people looking for donors of color, an analysis of other sperm banks’ donor registries reveals. The sperm bank Julie and Karen used has just 23 black donors and 30 Latino donors.
